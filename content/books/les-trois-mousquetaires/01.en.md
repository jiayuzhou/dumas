# The Three Gifts of M. d’Artagnan’s Father

@en-010 On the first Monday of April 1625, the town of Meung, where the author of the _Romance of the Rose_ was born, seemed to be in an uprising.
@en-020 Several burghers, seeing the women flee toward the High Street and hearing children cry out on the thresholds, hurried to strap on a cuirass.
@en-030 Steadying their uncertain bearing with a musket or a halberd, they headed toward the Franc-Meunier inn.
@en-040 There, minute by minute, a noisy, curious crowd thickened.
@en-050 In those days panics were common, and scarcely a day passed without some town recording an incident of that kind.

@en-060 The town flocked to the Franc-Meunier inn to see the cause of the uproar.
@en-070 There they remarked a young man with the air of Don Quixote stripped of armor, in a faded blue doublet tending toward claret and sky.
@en-080 His long, dark face, jutting cheekbones, Gascon jaw muscles, bright eye, and finely drawn nose all betrayed his province.
@en-090 Too tall for a youth, too short for a grown man, he would have passed for a farmer’s son but for the long sword beating his calves.
@en-100 His Béarn pony, yellow-coated, maned as good as none and galled with windgalls, carried its head lower than its knees and still made eight leagues a day.
@en-110 The appearance of the nag produced an unfavorable sensation that reflected on the rider.
@en-120 D’Artagnan—that was his name—smarted at the ridicule such a beast brought him, good horseman though he was.
@en-130 He sighed as he accepted M. d’Artagnan senior’s gift, knowing the horse was worth twenty livres and the words that came with it more.
@en-140 His father urged him to love the horse born in the house, spare the animal, and above all uphold his name with dignity at court.
@en-150 He enjoined him to suffer only the king and the cardinal, to be brave on every occasion, and to fight all the more because duels were forbidden.
@en-160 He had only fifteen crowns, the horse, and advice to give him; his mother added the recipe of a balm that cured every wound that spared the heart.
@en-170 They cited the example of M. de Tréville, once a neighbor, raised to captain of the musketeers, a model of courage and fortune.
@en-180 After blessings and tears, the young man set out with the three paternal gifts and the advice into the bargain.
@en-190 An exact copy of Don Quixote, he took every smile for an insult and every glance for a provocation.
@en-200 From Tarbes to Meung his fist stayed clenched and his hand ten times a day on the hilt; yet he struck no jaw and drew no blade.
@en-210 The sight of the yellow nag inspired laughter, but the respectable sword and fierce eye kept it in check.
@en-220 He remained majestic and unimpaired in his touchiness up to the ill-fated town of Meung.
@en-230 Dismounting at the Franc-Meunier, he saw at a window a tall, high-bred gentleman who seemed to mock his horse.
@en-240 The listeners laughed loudly, which set the powder alight.
@en-250 D’Artagnan took stock of the stranger: fortyish, black piercing eyes, pale complexion, black moustache, violet doublet and hose creased like from a long journey.
@en-260 Observation made, he hurried to the stable, entrusted his mount, and returned to the bench beneath the window.
@en-270 He heard the gentleman advise sending that horse to the king’s stables as a model, so singular was it.
@en-280 The laughter redoubled; d’Artagnan’s hand went to his sword.
@en-290 A haughty look from the stranger stopped him short and nailed him in place; the blood rushed to his face.
@en-300 He drew his blade an inch and swore the unknown would pay for such mockery.
@en-310 But the stranger vanished; d’Artagnan rushed to pursue him and was held back by the host.
@en-320 He learned only that the gentleman was taking the road to Paris at once.
@en-330 D’Artagnan swore to overtake him, settled with the innkeeper, swallowed a little bread and wine, and set off at a gallop.
@en-340 A quarter league on, he sighted the stranger and tried to provoke him, but was roughly handled by three men of his suite.
@en-350 He took so hard a blow that he fell half-stunned and saw the unknown disappear.
@en-360 Coming to, bruised, he picked up his poor bent sword and, furious and ashamed, resumed the road to Paris.
@en-370 Toward evening he found a room near the rue du Vieux-Colombier and vowed to find M. de Tréville in the morning.
@en-380 Pleased with his conduct at Meung despite everything, confident and full of hope, he fell asleep the sleep of the brave.
@en-390 In the morning he rose at nine to go to that famous M. de Tréville, the third personage in the realm by his father’s reckoning.
